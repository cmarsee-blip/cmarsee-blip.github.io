A deadline I couldn't extend

Two weeks into TripleTen, I found out my wife was pregnant with our fourth son. Most of a software engineering program still stood between me and the finish line, and now there was a due date on the other end of it that wasn't going to move for anyone's sprint schedule. That's the moment the program stopped being something I was working through and became something I had to plan around.

The rule I made on day one

I set one boundary and didn't break it: I only studied and worked on assignments during downtime at my day job or late at night after the family was asleep. Family time stayed family time, with one exception — if an instructor meeting landed during those hours, I took it. That was the only carve-out I allowed myself, and it turned out to be enough. The rule wasn't really about willpower. It was about making the decision once, up front, so I wasn't renegotiating it every single day.

Running ahead, on purpose

TripleTen's program allows up to 19 weeks of extensions, requested a week at a time. I never used any of them — not because I was against the idea, but because I'd rather build margin than borrow it. At a couple of points I was a month and a half ahead of schedule. That buffer wasn't padding for its own sake; it was insurance for a due date I couldn't negotiate with.

Extensions were always available. I just didn't want to need them.

The last two weeks

The buffer I'd built up mostly evaporated in the final stretch, the way it always does — sprints get harder as you go, not easier. I ended up finishing about a week ahead of schedule, which put the very last day of the program two days before my son was born. That timing wasn't luck; it was a direct return on every late night earlier in the program that I hadn't strictly needed to work but did anyway.

The teammate who made it possible

None of this was solo. My wife carried a lot more than the pregnancy during those months — she held the household together on the nights I was heads-down after the kids were in bed, and she was every bit as invested in me finishing as I was. If there's a single biggest factor in finishing early, it's not my schedule. It's that I had a real teammate running the same play I was.

I think about that stretch often now, sitting at a desk instead of a construction site. The tools changed. The habits didn't — build in margin before you need it, protect the boundaries that matter, and don't try to do it without a good team behind you.
