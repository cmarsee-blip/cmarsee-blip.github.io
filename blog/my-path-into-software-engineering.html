My path into software engineering

My route into this field didn't start anywhere near a computer. If you'd told the version of me coaching third base ten years ago that I'd end up writing JavaScript for a living, I'd have asked what that was.

First inning: sports

I grew up playing sports and never really left them. I coached, managed facilities, and eventually earned a Bachelor's in Sports Management with a Business minor. It was hands-on work — a lot of problem-solving in real time, a lot of managing people and moving parts under pressure. I didn't know it yet, but that's most of what software engineering turns out to be too.

Extra innings: construction

When my kids came along, I needed something steadier, so I moved into construction management as a Project Inspector for INDOT. The job was about precision — checking work against a spec, catching problems before they became expensive ones, documenting everything so there was no ambiguity later. Again, more transferable than I realized at the time: reading a spec closely and verifying the work matches it is basically debugging with a hard hat on.

Stepping up to the plate: software

Technology had been pulling at my curiosity in the background the whole time. I finally stopped putting it off, looked into what it would take to make the switch, and found TripleTen's software engineering program. I went through the full sequence — HTML and CSS fundamentals, vanilla JavaScript and DOM manipulation, React, then a full backend with Express and MongoDB — building a real project at every stage instead of just reading about one.

The projects on this site aren't tutorials I followed — they're things I built, broke, and fixed until they worked.

Where I'm at now

I'm job hunting and open to opportunities as a software engineer. Outside of that, not much has actually changed — I'm still coaching, still playing when I get the chance, and I'm a husband and father of four sons who somehow also found time to get into 3D printing this past year. I'm also a certified Firefighter I & II with First Aid/CPR training, which is its own kind of lesson in staying level-headed when something breaks and everyone's looking at you to fix it.

If any of this resonates or you want to talk shop, my contact info is at the bottom of the site — I'd genuinely like to hear from you.
